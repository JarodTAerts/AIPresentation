AI art — creative power, contested ethics

Generative art lowers the barrier to making images, music, and video. It also redistributes creative value — often without creators choosing the terms.

✍️
Consent & compensation
Training can absorb a creator's work without an individual opt-in. Licensing, collective payment, and machine-readable “do not train” signals are emerging — but remain incomplete.
📉
More images; fewer human sellers
A Stanford marketplace study found AI entry raised image sales 39% while the number of non-AI sellers fell about 23%.
🪪
Identity & provenance
Style, face, and voice can be imitated at scale. C2PA Content Credentials can record origin and edits, but provenance can be stripped or lost across platforms.
🌍
Access vs. cultural averaging
New tools empower people without formal training or physical access to traditional media. Mainstream-heavy datasets can also flatten aesthetics and underrepresent smaller cultures.

Both can be true: AI can democratize creative expression while concentrating platform power and weakening the bargaining position of working artists.

Sources: U.S. Copyright Office AI reports (2025) · Stanford GSB creative-marketplace study (2025) · UNESCO AI & Culture report (2025) · C2PA specification v2.4.
